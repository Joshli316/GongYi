# The 11 Most Common OPT Mistakes F-1 Students Make (And How to Avoid Them)

> A comprehensive guide to the pitfalls that cause F-1 students to lose status, face bars to reentry, or forfeit work authorization during Optional Practical Training.

---

## Overview

Optional Practical Training is one of the most valuable benefits of F-1 status, but it comes with strict rules that are easy to misunderstand. A single mistake can result in SEVIS termination, unauthorized employment, or a permanent immigration record that affects future visa applications. This report covers the 11 most common OPT mistakes, their consequences, and how to prevent each one.

---

## Mistake 1: Miscounting Unemployment Days

**The Rule:** F-1 students on post-completion OPT may not accumulate more than **90 aggregate days** of unemployment. STEM OPT extends this to **150 total days** (not an additional 150 -- it includes the initial OPT period).

**Common Errors:**

| Scenario | What Students Think | What Actually Happens |
|---|---|---|
| EAD start date is June 1, but student doesn't start working until June 20 | "My clock starts when I begin working" | 19 unemployment days accrued from day one |
| Working 15 hours/week | "I have a job, so I'm not unemployed" | Work under 20 hours/week **still counts as unemployment** |
| Leaving the U.S. for 3 weeks while unemployed | "Days outside the U.S. don't count" | Unemployment days accrue even while abroad |
| Volunteer work at a church, 10 hours/week | "Volunteering counts as employment" | General volunteering does not stop the clock |

**Consequences:** Exceeding the 90-day (or 150-day) limit triggers automatic SEVIS termination. SEVP will terminate your record within 15 days of detecting the violation. Once terminated, you are out of status and must depart the U.S. or face accrual of unlawful presence.

**Prevention:**
- Track unemployment days from the EAD start date, not your first day of work
- Ensure all employment is at least 20 hours/week and related to your field of study
- Report every job to your DSO immediately so it appears in SEVIS
- Keep a personal spreadsheet of employment start/end dates as backup

## Mistake 2: Missing Application Deadlines

**The Rule:** Post-completion OPT applications may be filed up to **90 days before** your program end date and must be received by USCIS no later than **60 days after** the program end date. Additionally, USCIS must receive the Form I-765 within **30 days** of your DSO entering the OPT recommendation in SEVIS.

**Common Errors:**

- Waiting until after graduation to start the process (your DSO needs time to issue the I-20)
- Confusing the 60-day grace period with the OPT application window
- Submitting with the wrong fee amount (the fee increased to **$1,780** in 2026)
- Using an outdated version of Form I-765
- Missing required biometrics appointment (as of December 2025, self-submitted photos are no longer accepted -- USCIS requires an in-person biometrics appointment)

**Consequences:** A late application is automatically denied. If denied after the 60-day grace period has expired, you are immediately out of status with no recourse. Incorrect fees or forms result in rejection (not denial), which consumes precious days from your filing window.

**Prevention:**
- Start the OPT process with your DSO **at least 90 days** before your program end date
- Confirm the current filing fee on the USCIS website before submitting
- Download the latest Form I-765 directly from uscis.gov
- Track every deadline on a calendar with reminders at 30, 14, and 7 days out
- Submit by mail with tracking or file online if available

## Mistake 3: Not Reporting Employment Changes to Your DSO

**The Rule:** Federal regulations (**8 CFR 214.2(f)(12)(ii)**) require F-1 students on post-completion OPT to report any change in employment information to their DSO **within 10 days**. The DSO must then update SEVIS within 21 days.

**What Must Be Reported:**

- Starting a new job
- Changing employers
- Adding a second employer
- Ending employment (voluntary or involuntary)
- Change in job title or duties
- Change from full-time to part-time (or vice versa)
- Change of employer name or address

**Consequences:** Failure to report within 10 days can result in SEVIS termination. Days without an employer logged in SEVIS count toward your unemployment limit. If SEVIS shows no employer and your 90 days run out, automatic termination follows -- even if you were actually employed the entire time.

**Prevention:**
- Report every employment change to your DSO within **48 hours** (well inside the 10-day window)
- Keep copies of offer letters, employment verification letters, and pay stubs
- Confirm with your DSO that the update appears in SEVIS
- Set a recurring monthly reminder to verify your SEVIS employment record is current

## Mistake 4: Working Without an EAD Card in Hand

**The Rule:** You may not begin employment until **(a)** USCIS has approved your Form I-765, **(b)** you have physically received your EAD card, and **(c)** the start date printed on the EAD has arrived. All three conditions must be met.

**Common Errors:**

- Starting work based on a verbal approval or receipt notice (I-797)
- Assuming the employer can "start you early" and backdate paperwork
- Working during the gap between program end date and EAD approval
- Relying on an EAD from a previous OPT period

**Consequences:** Working before your EAD start date or without the physical card constitutes **unauthorized employment** -- one of the most serious F-1 violations. This can result in:

- Immediate termination of F-1 status
- Accrual of unlawful presence
- Potential 3-year or 10-year bar on reentry to the U.S.
- Ineligibility for future visa benefits

**Prevention:**
- Never begin any form of employment (paid or unpaid) until you have the physical EAD card and the start date has passed
- If your employer pressures you to start early, explain the legal consequences and share USCIS guidance
- If processing is delayed, consult your DSO about expedite options or contact USCIS

## Mistake 5: Working for a Non-E-Verify Employer on STEM OPT

**The Rule:** The 24-month STEM OPT extension requires your employer to be enrolled in **E-Verify** at the time of filing. This is a strict eligibility requirement -- not discretionary.

**Common Errors:**

- Assuming the employer will enroll in E-Verify "soon" and filing anyway
- Not verifying E-Verify enrollment independently (there is no public database)
- Confusing E-Verify with I-9 compliance (they are separate systems)
- Accepting verbal assurance from HR without documentation
- Not realizing E-Verify enrollment must be at the **company level**, not just the hiring site

**Consequences:** USCIS will deny the STEM OPT application if the employer is not enrolled in E-Verify. A denial during the gap between initial OPT expiration and STEM OPT can leave you without work authorization and potentially out of status.

**Prevention:**
- Ask the employer for their **E-Verify Company ID number** and the date of their Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with DHS
- Request written confirmation of E-Verify enrollment before filing
- If the employer is not enrolled, they must complete enrollment **before** the I-765 is filed
- Remember: E-Verify is a requirement for the employer, not the student -- but it is the student who suffers the consequences of non-compliance

## Mistake 6: Not Maintaining a Valid I-20

**The Rule:** Your I-20 is the foundational document of your F-1 status. During OPT, you must have an I-20 that reflects your current OPT authorization, correct personal information, and a valid travel signature (if traveling).

**Common Errors:**

- Not requesting an updated I-20 after OPT approval
- Traveling with an I-20 that has an expired travel signature (must be less than 6 months old)
- Not updating the I-20 when changing employers or addresses
- Losing the I-20 and not requesting a replacement

**Consequences:** An invalid or outdated I-20 can result in denial of reentry at the border, inability to prove valid status to employers, and complications with future immigration applications.

**Prevention:**
- Request a new travel signature from your DSO every **6 months** while on OPT
- Keep your I-20 in a safe location (and maintain a digital scan as backup)
- Contact your DSO whenever your personal, academic, or employment information changes
- Never travel internationally without checking that your I-20 has a current travel signature

## Mistake 7: Traveling Without Proper Documents

**The Rule:** Travel during OPT is permitted but risky. Reentry requires a specific set of documents, and missing any one can result in being denied entry.

**Required Documents for Reentry During OPT:**

| Document | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Valid passport | Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond reentry date |
| Valid F-1 visa stamp | Must be unexpired (Canadian citizens exempt) |
| I-20 with travel signature | Signed by DSO within the last 6 months |
| EAD card | Must be valid (note: EAD says "not valid for reentry" -- you still need it) |
| Employment evidence | Offer letter, employment verification, or pay stubs showing active job |
| I-797 receipt (if pending) | If STEM OPT or other application is pending |

**Common Errors:**

- Traveling while OPT application is pending (high risk -- if denied while abroad, you cannot reenter)
- Traveling without an active job or job offer (CBP may question your intent)
- Assuming an expired F-1 visa stamp is acceptable for reentry (it is not, unless you qualify for automatic visa revalidation from Canada/Mexico for trips under 30 days)
- Traveling during the 60-day grace period after OPT ends (you cannot reenter during this period)
- Traveling during cap-gap (leaving the U.S. terminates cap-gap protection)

**Consequences:** Denied reentry means you cannot return to the U.S. in F-1 status. If your F-1 visa has expired, you must obtain a new visa at a consulate before returning, and consulates may deny the visa if they believe you lack intent to return home.

**Prevention:**
- Avoid international travel while any USCIS application is pending
- Never travel during the 60-day grace period or during cap-gap
- Carry all documents listed above plus copies of recent pay stubs
- Consult your DSO before booking any international travel

## Mistake 8: Failing to Report Address Changes

**The Rule:** F-1 students must report any change of address to their DSO **within 10 days** of moving. This is a federal requirement under **8 CFR 214.1(f)**. Separately, all non-citizens must report address changes to USCIS within 10 days via **Form AR-11** or through the SEVIS update (if your DSO updates SEVIS within 21 days, a separate AR-11 filing is not required).

**Common Errors:**

- Moving and forgetting to notify the DSO (especially after graduation when you feel "done" with school)
- Assuming USCIS will update automatically when you file a change of address with the post office
- Not realizing the 10-day deadline applies to temporary moves as well
- Filing AR-11 but not notifying the DSO (or vice versa)

**Consequences:** Failure to report an address change is a violation of status and can result in SEVIS termination. It can also cause you to miss critical USCIS correspondence (approval notices, RFEs, denial notices) that is sent to your address of record.

**Prevention:**
- Notify your DSO of any address change within **48 hours** (well before the 10-day deadline)
- Confirm your DSO has updated SEVIS
- If unsure whether SEVIS was updated in time, file Form AR-11 online at uscis.gov as a backup
- Keep your USCIS online account address current if you have pending applications

## Mistake 9: Confusing Pre-Completion and Post-Completion OPT Rules

**The Rule:** Pre-completion OPT and post-completion OPT have fundamentally different rules for timing, hours, and reporting.

| Rule | Pre-Completion OPT | Post-Completion OPT |
|---|---|---|
| When | Before degree completion | After degree completion |
| Hours (during school) | 20 hours/week maximum | 20+ hours/week minimum |
| Hours (during breaks) | Full-time allowed | Full-time allowed |
| Unemployment limit | None | 90 days (150 for STEM) |
| Employment reporting | Not required via SEVIS | Required within 10 days |
| Impact on post-completion OPT | Deducts from 12-month total | N/A |

**The Critical Trap:** Full-time pre-completion OPT is deducted from post-completion OPT at a **1:1 ratio**. Part-time pre-completion OPT (20 hours/week or less) is deducted at a **0.5:1 ratio**. Example: 6 months of part-time pre-completion OPT reduces your post-completion OPT to 9 months (12 - 3 = 9).

**Another Trap:** Using **12 months of full-time CPT** (Curricular Practical Training) disqualifies you from OPT entirely. Students sometimes confuse CPT and pre-completion OPT rules.

**Prevention:**
- Consult your DSO before using any pre-completion OPT to understand the impact on your post-completion eligibility
- Track all pre-completion OPT usage carefully (dates, hours per week)
- Never assume CPT and OPT rules are interchangeable

## Mistake 10: Not Understanding Cap-Gap Rules

**The Rule:** The "cap-gap" automatically extends F-1 status (and sometimes OPT work authorization) for students whose employers file a timely, cap-subject H-1B petition requesting **change of status**. The extension bridges the gap between OPT expiration and the October 1 H-1B start date.

**Eligibility Requirements:**

- The H-1B petition must be **cap-subject** (not cap-exempt)
- The petition must request **change of status** (not consular processing)
- The petition must be filed during the H-1B filing period (beginning April 1)
- The student must be in valid F-1 status at the time of filing

**Common Errors:**

| Error | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Assuming cap-gap applies to consular processing petitions | Only change-of-status petitions qualify |
| Traveling outside the U.S. during cap-gap | Leaving the U.S. terminates cap-gap protection |
| Working during cap-gap when only status (not EAD) is extended | If the H-1B petition is denied or withdrawn, work authorization ends immediately |
| Assuming cap-gap is automatic without SEVIS confirmation | Your DSO must request the cap-gap extension in SEVIS |
| Not understanding what happens if H-1B is denied | You must stop working and depart within 60 days |

**Consequences:** Working without authorization during cap-gap (e.g., when only status but not employment authorization is extended) constitutes unauthorized employment. Traveling during cap-gap voids the extension entirely.

**Prevention:**
- Confirm with your DSO that the cap-gap extension has been entered in SEVIS
- Do not travel internationally during cap-gap under any circumstances
- Clarify with your attorney whether your specific situation extends both status and employment authorization, or status only
- Have a contingency plan if the H-1B petition is denied

## Mistake 11: Starting Work Before the OPT Start Date

**The Rule:** Your OPT employment authorization begins on the **start date printed on your EAD card** -- not the date the card is approved, not the date you receive it, and not the date your program ends.

**Common Errors:**

- Starting a "trial period" or "orientation" before the EAD start date
- Doing unpaid work for the future employer before OPT begins
- Assuming remote/freelance work from home is acceptable before the start date
- Confusing the I-20 OPT recommendation date with the EAD authorization date

**Consequences:** Any work performed before the EAD start date is unauthorized employment, carrying the same severe consequences as Mistake 4: status termination, unlawful presence, and potential bars to future U.S. entry.

**Prevention:**
- Communicate your exact EAD start date to your employer in writing
- Do not perform any work -- paid or unpaid, in-person or remote -- before that date
- If your employer needs you to start before OPT begins, explore whether CPT authorization is available through your academic program

## Top 5 Prevention Habits

1. **Build a personal timeline.** Map every deadline (application window, EAD start date, unemployment limit, reporting deadlines) on a calendar with alerts.
2. **Report early, report often.** Notify your DSO of any change within 48 hours -- don't wait for the 10-day deadline.
3. **Keep a paper trail.** Save copies of every I-20, EAD, offer letter, pay stub, and USCIS receipt in both physical and digital form.
4. **Consult before acting.** Talk to your DSO or an immigration attorney before traveling, changing jobs, or making any decision that affects your employment authorization.
5. **Never assume.** OPT rules are not intuitive. What seems reasonable (working a few days early, traveling briefly, volunteering) can constitute a serious violation.
